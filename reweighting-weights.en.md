# Reweighting Between a Biased and an Unbiased Ensemble

> A cleaned-up write-up of handwritten notes. It derives the importance-sampling
> weight that converts samples drawn from a *biased* Boltzmann distribution back
> into expectation values under the *unbiased* target distribution.

---

## 1. Ergodic hypothesis and the law of large numbers

Two classical results justify estimating an ensemble average from a finite
simulation.

**Ergodic hypothesis.** For a sufficiently long trajectory of a dynamical
system, the *time* average of an observable equals its *ensemble* average. For an
observable $A(\mathbf{x})$ on phase space, the time average along a trajectory
$\mathbf{x}(t)$ is

$$
\overline{A}
= \lim_{T \to \infty} \frac{1}{T} \int_{0}^{T} A(\mathbf{x}(t)) dt,
$$

while the ensemble average is

$$
\langle A\rangle = \int A(\mathbf{x}) \rho(\mathbf{x}) d\mathbf{x},
$$

with $\rho(\mathbf{x})$ the equilibrium distribution in phase space. The hypothesis
asserts

$$
\overline{A} = \langle A\rangle
$$

whenever the system is ergodic — the trajectory explores the accessible phase
space according to $\rho$. Because time is homogeneous, every point along the
trajectory carries equal weight; this is what lets molecular-dynamics time
averages stand in for thermodynamic ensemble averages.

**Law of large numbers.** For independent, identically distributed random
variables $\{X_i\}_{i=1}^{N}$ with finite expectation $\mathbb{E}[X]$, the sample
average converges to that expectation,

$$
\overline{X}_N = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} X_i
 \xrightarrow[N \to \infty]{}  \mathbb{E}[X].
$$

This is what makes a *finite* sample a legitimate estimator of an average.

Combining the two, an ensemble average of an observable can be estimated from the
configurations $\{\mathbf{x}_i\}$ generated by a simulation:

$$
\langle A\rangle = \int A(\mathbf{x}) \rho(\mathbf{x}) d\mathbf{x}
 \approx  \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} A(\mathbf{x}_i).
$$

**Free energy is different.** This estimator works only for genuine observables.
Free energy is *not* an observable: it depends on the partition function — a
high-dimensional integral that is not accessible from a normalized sampling
distribution, since the probability density itself is defined through that
partition function, and averaging the density is not physically meaningful. This
gap is exactly what reweighting addresses: the reweighted estimator of §4 is the
sample average above with each term multiplied by an importance weight, and the
unknown normalization enters only as a partition-function ratio $c_b/c$ (§4–§5),
which cancels once the weights are normalized. It reduces to the plain average
here when the bias $b\equiv 0$.

---

## 2. Setup

We work with configurations $\mathbf{x}$ (a point in configuration space). Two
Boltzmann-type probability densities are compared.

**Target (unbiased) distribution.** With reduced potential $u_0(\mathbf{x})$,

$$
p(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{c} \exp\big(-u_0(\mathbf{x})\big),
\qquad
c = \int \exp\big(-u_0(\mathbf{x})\big) d\mathbf{x} .
$$

Here $c$ is the partition function that normalizes $p$ to unit integral.

**Biased distribution.** A bias potential $b(\mathbf{x})$ is added to $u_0$,

$$
p_b(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{c_b} \exp\big(-u_0(\mathbf{x}) - b(\mathbf{x})\big),
\qquad
c_b = \int \exp\big(-u_0(\mathbf{x}) - b(\mathbf{x})\big) d\mathbf{x} .
$$

Sampling (e.g. an enhanced-sampling or umbrella-sampling simulation) is carried
out under $p_b$; we want averages under $p$.

These two lines are the first two lines of the notes.

---

## 3. The reweighting factor

To recover an unbiased average from biased samples we need the pointwise ratio
of the two densities — the **importance weight**:

$$
w(\mathbf{x})  =  \frac{p(\mathbf{x})}{p_b(\mathbf{x})}
 = 
\frac{\dfrac{1}{c} \exp\big(-u_0(\mathbf{x})\big)}
     {\dfrac{1}{c_b} \exp\big(-u_0(\mathbf{x}) - b(\mathbf{x})\big)} .
$$

The exponential factors combine through their arguments,

$$
-u_0(\mathbf{x})  -  \big(-u_0(\mathbf{x}) - b(\mathbf{x})\big)  =  b(\mathbf{x}),
$$

so the shared $-u_0(\mathbf{x})$ cancels and only the bias survives in the exponent:

$$
w(\mathbf{x})
 = 
\frac{1}{c}\cdot\frac{\exp\big(b(\mathbf{x})\big)}{ 1/c_b }
 = 
\boxed{ \frac{c_b}{c} \exp\big(b(\mathbf{x})\big)  }.
$$

This is the middle block of the notes. The weight is the product of a constant
prefactor $c_b/c$ (a ratio of partition functions) and the configuration-dependent
factor $\exp\big(b(\mathbf{x})\big)$, which simply undoes the bias that was applied.

---

## 4. Normalized weight

In practice the prefactor $c_b/c$ is unknown, because the partition functions are
not computed directly. What is used is the **self-normalized weight** $W(\mathbf{x})$,
in which the same ratio is written with the biased density $p_b$ (equivalently
$c_b^{-1}\exp(-u_0 - b)$) spelled out in the denominator — the "mbar"-style form
in the notes:

$$
W(\mathbf{x})
 = 
\frac{1}{c} 
\frac{\exp\big(-u_0(\mathbf{x})\big)}
     { c_b^{-1}\exp\big(-u_0(\mathbf{x}) - b(\mathbf{x})\big) }
 = 
\frac{c_b}{c} \exp\big(b(\mathbf{x})\big).
$$

Since the denominator is exactly $p_b(\mathbf{x})$, this reduces to the same result
as §3: $W(\mathbf{x}) = w(\mathbf{x}) = \dfrac{c_b}{c}\exp\big(b(\mathbf{x})\big)$. This is the
bottom line of the notes.

The unknown constant $c_b/c$ drops out whenever the weights are normalized over a
sample set $\{\mathbf{x}_i\}$,

$$
\widehat{W}(\mathbf{x}_i) = \frac{w(\mathbf{x}_i)}{\sum_j w(\mathbf{x}_j)}
= \frac{\exp\big(b(\mathbf{x}_i)\big)}{\sum_j \exp\big(b(\mathbf{x}_j)\big)},
$$

so an unbiased ensemble average of any observable $A$ can be estimated purely from
the bias values:

$$
\langle A\rangle_p  \approx  \sum_i \widehat{W}(\mathbf{x}_i) A(\mathbf{x}_i)
 =  \frac{\sum_i A(\mathbf{x}_i) e^{ b(\mathbf{x}_i)}}{\sum_i e^{ b(\mathbf{x}_i)}}.
$$

---

## 5. Remark

The whole derivation rests on one cancellation: because $p$ and $p_b$ share the
same underlying potential $u_0$, their ratio keeps no dependence on $u_0$ — only
the bias $b(\mathbf{x})$ and the constant partition-function ratio remain. That is
what makes reweighting from a biased simulation exact in principle: multiply each
biased sample by $e^{ b(\mathbf{x})}$ (up to overall normalization) and the unbiased
statistics are restored.
